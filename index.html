The Vision

Why I am running

My name is Dara-Abasi Ita, and I am running to lead our faculty on what I call the PEAK agenda. Four pillars, and beneath each one a set of things I mean to actually do, not merely say.

Professional Advancement. Equity and Inclusion. Academic Excellence. Kinship and Community. Here is what each of them means, and why it matters.

P
Pillar One

Professional Advancement

Most of us came to law school with the same quiet hope: that it would lead somewhere. Too often the path there is hidden, and the money to run our own affairs is never quite enough. This pillar is about opening that path and standing on our own feet.

Career & Internship Board

Right now, the openings that matter reach only the students who happen to be well connected. Someone hears of an internship, tells a friend, and the rest of us never knew it existed. I want to end that quiet unfairness. The Career and Internship Board will be one place where opportunities and internships are gathered and shared openly, so that a first-year with no contacts sees the same chance as anyone else. If you have the merit, you should not lose out simply because you did not know.

IGR Board

We cannot keep running this association on dues alone. It leaves us broke, and it leaves us begging. I want an Internally Generated Revenue Board whose whole job is to help us earn our own keep, so that when we plan something we are not stopped for want of money we could have raised ourselves. There is honest money in things we already need: ties carrying our logo, ID cards issued by us, corporate bags, brooches, diaries and pens. Run well, these fund the very programmes on this page, and they do it without reaching deeper into your pocket.

E
Pillar Two

Equity and Inclusion

A faculty should not quietly leave its poorest and most vulnerable behind. This pillar is about making sure that where you come from, or what you can afford, is never the thing that decides how far you go here.

Scholarship Board

Every year, students who belong here struggle to stay, not for want of ability but for want of money. I want a Scholarship Board that takes this seriously. The first thing it will do is organise our alumni by their graduating class, the Class of 2000, the Class of 2001, and so on, so that when we raise funds we approach a whole class rather than chasing individuals. That way the weight rests on many shoulders and barely touches any one of them. The scholarships themselves will be open to three groups: students living with disability; students of clear academic excellence who lack the means; and students who simply lack the means. And every naira will be accounted for in the open. We will publish what was raised, what has been spent, and what remains, so that trust is never something you have to take on faith.

Welfare

Beyond scholarships, equity is also the small daily dignity of being a student here, and that has been neglected for too long.

I will be the bridge between you and faculty management. Too many of our complaints die somewhere between the student who raised them and the office that could fix them. I intend to carry them properly, and to bring answers back.

I will put cleaners on a proper payroll, so that our toilets and recreational spaces are kept genuinely clean, not cleaned once and forgotten. Hygiene is not a luxury.

I will provide free examination materials each exam period, blue and red pens and two staplers for every class, so that no one sits an exam unprepared for want of something so cheap to give.

I will subsidise printing within the faculty, so that preparing for class stops quietly costing us money we do not have.

A
Pillar Three

Academic Excellence

We do our best work when the tools to succeed are within reach, and when the chances to shine are open to everyone rather than a chosen few. This pillar is about both.

E-Repository

Past questions and materials are among the most useful things a law student can have, and yet they circulate in private, reaching some of us and not others, often too late to help. I want to build an e-repository, a shared library much like a Google Drive, holding the past questions and materials for every class and every course, arranged year by year. Once, and for all of us. What has always been an advantage for the few becomes a right for everyone.

Voluntary Mock Examinations

Nothing prepares you for an exam like sitting one before it counts. I will organise voluntary mock examinations for classes that want them, so that we walk into the real thing already familiar with its shape, its pressure and its pace.

Competition Board

Our competitions have too often been closed rooms, the same names chosen by a process no one can see. That is not how merit is meant to work. I want a Competition Board that throws those rooms open. Selection will follow a clear, merit-based standard, and the process itself will be transparent and open to every student who qualifies. If you are good enough, you will get your chance, and you will be able to see exactly how you earned it.

K
Pillar Four

Kinship and Community

We have slowly become strangers to one another, and to the alumni who came before us. This pillar is about remembering that we are a people, not just a register of names.

LAWSAN Week

I want to bring back LAWSAN Week and make it the moment the whole faculty comes alive again. It will run across several days and gather all of us, past and present:

A casual cocktail and meet-and-greet, where we sit with our alumni as people rather than distant benefactors.

An academic and career conference, where those who have gone ahead of us tell us honestly how they did it.

A cultural day, where we celebrate everything we are and everywhere we come from.

A moot competition and a deal advisory competition, where our sharpest minds test themselves in the open.

A dinner ceremony to close, where we mark what we have done together and who we have become.

That is the PEAK agenda. Every line of it is something you will be able to hold me to, and something we can only reach together.
